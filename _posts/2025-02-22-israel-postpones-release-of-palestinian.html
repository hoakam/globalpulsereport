Israel postpones the release of Palestinian prisoners indefinitely as hostages are freed

 Israel has announced an indefinite postponement of the planned release of over 600 Palestinian prisoners, dealing a significant blow to fragile ceasefire negotiations. The decision follows the return on Saturday of six Israeli hostages, including four seized during Hamas’s October 7, 2023, assault on southern Israel, which killed approximately 1,200 people. Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu stated the prisoner release would remain stalled until Hamas guarantees further hostage handovers and halts what he described as “degrading propaganda spectacles” during weekly transfers. The first phase of the truce, set to expire soon, includes only one remaining exchange involving four hostages confirmed deceased in captivity. Talks for phase two—focused on freeing living captives—remain stalled, raising fears of a total collapse of the agreement.

Netanyahu accused Hamas of “repeated violations,” including manipulating hostages for political theater, after the group released Tal Shoham, Omer Shem Tov, Eliya Cohen, and Omer Wenkert—all taken from the Nova music festival—alongside long-term detainees Avera Mengistu and Hisham al-Sayed. Mengistu, an Ethiopian-Israeli with mental health struggles, had been held since 2014 after crossing into Gaza, while al-Sayed, a Bedouin Arab Israeli, entered the territory in 2015. Their quiet releases contrasted with Hamas’s publicized handovers of the Nova survivors, flanked by militants in staged scenes.

Meanwhile, Palestinian families gathered outside Ofer Prison in the West Bank, awaiting loved ones among the 555 detainees slated for release—50 serving life sentences, 60 with lengthy terms, and 445 arrested since October 7. An elderly mother in Gaza’s Khan Younis, whose son has spent 33 years in Israeli custody, told AFP she “can’t believe freedom is within reach.” Hamas swiftly condemned the delay as a breach of the deal, though it did not immediately respond to Netanyahu’s latest remarks.

The truce, mediated by Egypt and Qatar, began on January 19, with chaotic early exchanges evolving into orchestrated ceremonies. Saturday’s releases brought cautious relief to families in Tel Aviv’s Hostages Square, where crowds cheered live broadcasts of returnees. Relatives of the freed hostages, including Shoham’s family—whose wife and children were released earlier—urged swift action to secure the remaining 62 captives, around half presumed alive. Among them is 21-year-old Israeli-American soldier Edan Alexander, whose mother, Yael, called the wait “very tough” but expressed hope for a deal prioritizing living hostages.

The mood contrasted sharply with days earlier, when Israel recovered the bodies of Shiri Bibas, her two young sons, and Oded Lifschitz. Over 48,000 Palestinians, mostly civilians, have been killed in Israel’s retaliatory offensive, per Gaza’s Hamas-run health ministry.

As mediators scramble to salvage negotiations ahead of the ceasefire’s March 1 phase-two deadline, the impasse underscores deepening mistrust. Netanyahu faces pressure from hardliners opposed to concessions, while Hamas demands guarantees beyond prisoner swaps, including an end to Israeli military operations. For families on both sides, the stakes are agonizingly personal—a race against time to reunite with loved ones before diplomacy unravels into renewed violence.
