 Trump directs U.S. withdrawal from the leading global climate agreement.

The United States will be among just four countries not participating in the Paris Agreement, which aims to curb global greenhouse gas emissions.
In 2021, a coal-fired power plant in Kansas highlighted the challenges U.S. efforts to reduce emissions were facing, even before President Trump's announcement.

On Monday, President Trump signed an executive order withdrawing the United States from the Paris Agreement, a global pact aimed at combating climate change. This decision places the U.S. alongside Iran, Libya, and Yemen as one of the few nations not part of the accord, which seeks to prevent global warming from reaching catastrophic levels.

The withdrawal, part of a series of energy-related decisions announced shortly after his inauguration, marks another shift in the country’s stance on international climate agreements. Trump had previously pulled the U.S. out during his first term, only for President Biden to rejoin the pact in 2020 upon taking office.   

Critics, including scientists, activists, and Democratic officials, condemned the move, warning that it could exacerbate the climate crisis and negatively impact American workers. In conjunction with other energy policies unveiled on the same day, the decision underscores the administration’s commitment to increasing fossil fuel extraction and production while moving away from clean-energy advancements such as electric vehicles and wind power generation.
